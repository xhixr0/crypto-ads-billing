## Why Facebook Ads Keeps Declining Your Card in the First Place

Before we talk about any specific card, it helps to understand what Meta's payment system is actually doing when it rejects you. It's rarely random.

**The BIN problem.** Every card has a Bank Identification Number — the first six digits. Meta's fraud engine categorizes BINs by risk. Prepaid BINs, certain regions, certain issuer types — they get flagged. The Reddit thread on r/FacebookAds is full of media buyers who lost three ad accounts in a row because the card they were using had a "high-risk" BIN, even though the card itself was legitimate.

**Prepaid detection.** Meta officially says it accepts Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover, JCB. What it doesn't say out loud is that it regularly rejects prepaid and debit-only BINs. A card that works fine on Amazon can still get bounced by Ads Manager because Meta treats ad spend as a recurring billing risk — they're charging you continuously, and they want a "real" credit instrument, not a prepaid one.

**Country mismatch.** Your billing address is in Vietnam, your IP is in Lithuania, your card was issued in Hong Kong. Meta's anti-fraud layer reads that combination as suspicious even when it's innocent. This is the single most common reason a card that worked yesterday stops working today.

**Recurring international charges.** Even legitimate bank cards get flagged by their own issuers when Meta hits them with daily international charges that look "unusual" to the bank's pattern matching. The bank freezes the card, Meta sees the decline, the ad account goes into a billing hold, and you're stuck in support-ticket purgatory.

A crypto-funded Visa card doesn't fix everything, but it sidesteps a meaningful slice of these problems: you control the balance, the issuer is crypto-friendly by design, and the BIN tends to be a clean Visa one that Meta recognizes.

## Risk Avoidance: The Stuff That Gets Ad Accounts Killed

The Reddit r/FacebookAds thread on crypto cards is full of horror stories — three ad accounts dead in a week, balances locked, payment methods permanently restricted. Almost all of those stories trace back to a handful of mistakes. Here's the short list of what not to do.

**One card, one ad account.** Meta runs a "one card per account" anti-fraud rule. If you try to bind the same PokePay card to three ad accounts, expect at least two of them to get flagged. The fix is to issue multiple cards — and yes, PokePay supports this. Media buyers running serious volume typically maintain one virtual card per ad account, named per campaign, and close the card when the campaign ends.

**Don't use fake names or company names on the card.** Meta cross-checks the cardholder name against your ad account's identity. A mismatch is one of the fastest routes to a permanent restriction. Use your real name, in the same spelling PokePay has on file.

**Mind the IP environment.** If you're running multiple ad accounts, use a clean browser profile (something like VMLogin or Multilogin) with a residential proxy IP that matches your billing address country. Datacenter IPs from a random VPS are a red flag.

**Keep records.** Save your deposit confirmations, your card detail screenshots, your KYC document. When Meta does request documentation to lift a billing hold — and they will, eventually — having a clean paper trail cuts the resolution time from weeks to days.

**Don't panic-top-up.** If a charge fails, don't immediately retry five times. Each failed attempt is logged. Recharge the card, wait an hour, then retry once. If it still fails, switch to a backup card rather than burning through retries on the same one.
